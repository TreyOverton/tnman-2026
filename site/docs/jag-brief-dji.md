## Question Presented

Does any currently enforceable federal statute, Tennessee state statute, or known command directive prohibit a TNSG soldier from operating a personally-owned DJI drone in support of an official TNSG mission or training event, where the organization has not purchased or acquired that drone?

## Why This Matters for TNMAN-26

3 RGT's RFF requests **3 Part 107 sUAS operators**, of whom only **CPT Widner** is organic. The State Defense Force Foundation (SDFF) augmentation pool — see [Volunteer Pilots](/volunteer-pilots.md) — could close the gap, but a portion of those volunteers may bring **personally-owned DJI airframes**. The Section 889 / TCA § 12-4-120 / ASDA layering is not obvious from a single statute, and Mr. Armstrong (HAAP POC) has emphasized that **sUAS detection is the chief training objective** — implying drones will be flown. The unit needs a JAG opinion before equipment commitments are made.

## Federal Statutory Framework

### Section 889, NDAA FY2019 — The Foundational Ban

Section 889 is a **federal acquisition statute, not a general use prohibition**. It operates through two parts:

- **Part A** (eff. 13 Aug 2019) — Prohibits federal agencies from procuring or obtaining telecommunications equipment or services from covered entities (including DJI).
- **Part B** (eff. 13 Aug 2020) — Prohibits federal agencies from contracting with any entity that uses covered equipment in its operations, even if unrelated to the federal contract.

> **Section 889(a)(1)(A) — Core Prohibition** (Pub. L. 115-232 § 889):
> *"The head of an executive agency may not procure or obtain... any equipment, system, or service that uses covered telecommunications equipment or services as a substantial or essential component of any system, or as critical technology as part of any system."*

The reach is **acquisition and procurement**, not individual operational use by non-federal-contractor personnel. TNSG, as a state entity without federal-contractor status under the FAR, is not a regulated party under §889 in the procurement sense. Individual TNSG soldiers are not federal contractors.

**Unit conclusion:** §889 does not directly prohibit a TNSG soldier from operating a personally-owned DJI drone.

### American Security Drone Act (ASDA), FY2024 NDAA

The ASDA is the **first instrument that creates an operational use prohibition**. The implementing FAR rule became effective **22 December 2025**.

> **ASDA Core Prohibition** (41 U.S.C. 3901 note; FAR 52.240-1, eff. 22 Dec 2025):
> *Federal contractors are prohibited from using federal funds for the procurement or operation of a FASC-prohibited UAS. Agencies are prohibited from procuring services for the operation of a FASC-prohibited UAS.*

The operational use prohibition is **conditional** — it activates when both conditions are met:

1. The activity is performed under a federal contract, grant, or award.
2. Federal funds are involved.

For TNSG, the trigger question is **federal funding nexus**. State-only-funded TNSG activities are not covered. Federal-nexus missions (FEMA grant, Title 32 activation, federal emergency support funds, etc.) **are** covered — including a personally-owned DJI operated by a soldier in an official capacity in performance of that federally-funded activity.

**Unit conclusion:** ASDA creates a conditional operational use prohibition that activates with federal funding in the causal chain. **The determination is mission-specific.**

## Tennessee Statutory Framework

### TCA § 12-4-120 (SB776 / HB1070, enacted July 2023)

Tennessee enacted SB776 in 2023 to mirror Section 889 at the state level. The original bill prohibited three acts: **purchasing, acquiring, and "otherwise using"**. The final enacted statute prohibits only **two**.

| Version | Prohibited Acts |
|---------|-----------------|
| Original bill | purchasing, acquiring, **or otherwise using** |
| **Enacted (TCA § 12-4-120)** | **purchasing or acquiring** |

**Senate Amendment #1 (6 March 2023)** stripped *"otherwise using"* from the bill. The House passed the amended version 89-0 on 10 April 2023. *"Otherwise using"* was never restored.

> **TCA § 12-4-120 — As Enacted:**
> *A law enforcement agency, local agency, or state agency is prohibited from purchasing or acquiring a drone, as defined in the federal National Defense Authorization Act of 2019, produced by a manufacturer banned under Section 889 of the NDAA FY2019, as amended. A contract or agreement for the purchase or acquisition of a drone in violation of this section is void and unenforceable.*

The deliberate removal of *"otherwise using"* by Senate Amendment #1 is significant legislative history. Under standard statutory construction, courts do not read excised language back into a statute.

**Unit conclusion:** TCA § 12-4-120 is exclusively a procurement statute. It prohibits TNSG (as a state agency) from purchasing or acquiring DJI hardware. It does not prohibit operational use, and it does not reach a soldier's personally-owned equipment in either context. No subsequent Tennessee legislation has restored *"operational use"* language; no Tennessee court has expanded the scope.

## Judicial Posture

### *SZ DJI Technology Co., Ltd. v. U.S. Department of Defense*
*No. 1:2024-cv-02970 (D.D.C., September 2025)*

DJI sued in October 2024 challenging its designation as a "Chinese Military Company" under §1260H of the NDAA FY2021. Judge Friedman's 49-page opinion is instructive on two points:

- **What the court rejected:** No evidence DJI is owned or controlled by the CCP, affiliated with China's MIIT, or operating from a military-civil fusion zone.
- **What the court upheld:** The designation was sustained on two narrow grounds — DJI's status as a National Enterprise Technology Center (NETC), and DJI's dual-use technology profile. The court afforded DoD broad discretion in national-security listing decisions even without proof of direct CCP control.

DJI has appealed to the D.C. Circuit; the appeal is pending as of April 2026. **No stay has been granted; the designation and associated procurement restrictions remain in effect.**

**Relevance:** The D.D.C. ruling does not alter the statutory text of §889, TCA § 12-4-120, or ASDA. It addresses only the Chinese Military Company designation, a separate list from the §889 procurement blacklist.

### *Huawei Technologies Co. v. United States* (4:19-CV-00159, E.D. Tex. 2020)

District court rejected Huawei's bill-of-attainder challenge to §889. Procurement exclusions do not constitute "punishment" because they serve non-punitive national-security objectives. **Manufacturer-specific procurement bans are constitutionally sound.**

### *Kaspersky Lab, Inc. v. Department of Homeland Security* (841 F.3d 400, D.C. Cir. 2018)

D.C. Circuit upheld the FY2017 NDAA prohibition on government use of Kaspersky software. Exclusionary procurement restrictions based on national-security determinations are facially valid and do not require individualized judicial findings.

### *DJI v. FCC* (D.C. Cir., February 2026, pending)

DJI is separately challenging its placement on the FCC's Covered List. No order has stayed the listing. The FCC listing does not affect operation of already-authorized DJI hardware by private individuals.

### Summary

No federal or Tennessee court has issued a ruling that:

- Invalidates or narrows §889;
- Expands TCA § 12-4-120 beyond its enacted *"purchase or acquire"* text;
- Restores *"otherwise using"* to the Tennessee statute;
- Creates a carve-out for personal equipment used in official capacities;
- Grants injunctive relief that would suspend any applicable restriction.

**The litigation trajectory runs against DJI on every front where a ruling has been issued.**

## Unit Findings by Scenario

| # | Scenario | Finding | Legal Basis |
|---|----------|---------|-------------|
| 1 | Soldier owns DJI; uses recreationally off-duty | **NOT PROHIBITED** | No statute restricts personal civilian use |
| 2 | TNSG organization purchases or acquires a DJI drone | **PROHIBITED** | TCA § 12-4-120; §889 |
| 3 | Soldier brings personal DJI; operates during a **state-funded** TNSG mission in official capacity | **NOT PROHIBITED** (by statute) | TCA § 12-4-120 covers purchase/acquire only; *"otherwise using"* removed by amendment |
| 4 | Soldier brings personal DJI; operates during a **federally-funded** mission (Title 32, FEMA grant, etc.) | **CONTEXT-DEPENDENT** | ASDA / FAR 52.240-1 (Dec 2025) prohibits operation on federally-funded work |
| 5 | TNSG officially tasks a soldier to fly their personal DJI as a mission asset | **CONTEXT-DEPENDENT** | State-only funding: not prohibited by statute. Federal nexus: ASDA applies |

## Factors Outside Unit Visibility

The statutory and judicial research is complete to the best of the unit's ability. The following policy layers are **not accessible to the unit** and could change any of the above conclusions:

- NGB / ARNG UAS security policy directives that may impose operational use restrictions beyond statutory minimums
- DoD UAS Cybersecurity requirements applicable to NG units in federal status
- TAG-level or TNAG-issued directives restricting use of foreign-manufactured UAS by TNSG personnel
- Any TNSG SOP, standing order, or command policy addressing personally-owned equipment used during official activities
- OPSEC or intelligence community guidance that may functionally prohibit DJI use in certain mission sets regardless of legal status

Any one of these could operationally prohibit what the statutes do not, without contradicting the statutory analysis above.

## Request for JAG Confirmation

The unit respectfully requests JAG confirm, correct, or qualify the following:

1. **§889 (FY2019 NDAA)** does not prohibit TNSG soldiers from operating personally-owned DJI hardware — it is a federal acquisition statute that applies to procurement and federal contracting relationships, not to state-entity personnel operating personal equipment.
2. **TCA § 12-4-120** prohibits TNSG from purchasing or acquiring DJI hardware but does not prohibit operational use, based on the enacted text and the deliberate removal of *"otherwise using"* by Senate Amendment #1.
3. **ASDA** creates an operational-use prohibition tied to federal funding. For TNSG missions or exercises with a federal funding nexus, a soldier operating a personally-owned DJI in an official capacity would likely be prohibited under ASDA effective 22 December 2025.
4. **No judicial ruling** has modified, narrowed, or expanded any of the above provisions in a way that would change these conclusions.

**Please advise** whether any NGB directive, DoD UAS policy, TAG-level order, or TNSG internal policy imposes operational use restrictions beyond the above statutes that would further limit or prohibit a soldier's use of personally-owned DJI hardware during official TNSG activities.
