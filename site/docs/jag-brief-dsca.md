# JAG Pre-Brief: DSCA, "Civilian Support of Military Operations," and TNSG Authority for TNMAN

**Prepared for:** Staff Judge Advocate
**Subject:** Clarification of doctrinal framework and statutory authority underlying TNSG participation in TNMAN
**Status:** Pre-brief / request for legal review
**Disclaimer:** The undersigned are not attorneys. This document reflects open-source research and a working hypothesis. We are surfacing it specifically so that JAG can advise authoritatively, correct any errors, and — if warranted — help us clean up the mission/authority framing in the TNMAN planning record.

---

## 1. Questions Presented

We respectfully ask JAG to address the following:

1. **Definition.** Does the DoD doctrinal concept of *Defense Support of Civil Authorities* (DSCA), as defined in DoDD 3025.18 and JP 3-28, encompass anything properly described as "civilian support of military operations"?

2. **Scope of "civilians" in DSCA.** Within the DSCA framework, when the term "civilians" appears (e.g., "DoD civilians" in the DSCA definition), to whom does it refer? Specifically:
   - Federal civilian employees of DoD components?
   - Civilian Expeditionary Workforce (CEW) personnel?
   - DoD contractors and contractors authorized to accompany the force (CAAF)?
   - Non-federally employed or non-federally funded personnel (e.g., state employees, private citizens, volunteer state defense force members)?

3. **Underlying basis of the phrase.** What is the doctrinal or statutory basis, if any, for the phrase "civilian support of military operations" as TNMAN planners have used it? Does it appear in any controlling DoD issuance, joint publication, or federal statute we should be reviewing?

4. **Application to TNSG.** Does any DSCA-derived authority apply to the Tennessee State Guard — an unpaid, all-volunteer state defense force organized under Article I, § 24 of the Tennessee Constitution and TCA Title 58 — when participating in TNMAN? Or does TNSG's authority to participate flow exclusively from state law and 32 U.S.C. § 109?

5. **HD vs. DSCA framing.** A staff comment in planning to the effect that *"the mission of DSCA is not appropriate in a homeland defense scenario"* appears doctrinally well-founded based on JP 3-27 / JP 3-28. Does JAG concur that DSCA and Homeland Defense (HD) are distinct mission frameworks with different lead/supporting relationships, and that conflating them is a category error we should correct in the planning record?

## 2. Background and Research Findings

**Our research suggests** the following, subject to JAG's correction:

### 2.1 DSCA stands for "Defense Support of Civil Authorities"

The controlling DoD issuance is **DoD Directive 3025.18, *Defense Support of Civil Authorities*** (29 December 2010, with subsequent change). The implementing regulation is codified at **32 C.F.R. Part 185**. Both define DSCA as:

> Support provided by U.S. Federal military forces, DoD civilians, DoD contract personnel, DoD Component assets, and National Guard forces (when the Secretary of Defense, in coordination with the Governors of the affected States, elects and requests to use those forces in title 32, U.S.C., status) in response to requests for assistance from civil authorities for domestic emergencies, law enforcement support, and other domestic activities, or from qualifying entities for special events. Also known as civil support.
>
> — DoDD 3025.18, Glossary; 32 C.F.R. § 185.3

The semantic vector of DSCA is unambiguous: the **military supports civil authorities**. Civil authorities lead; DoD supports.

### 2.2 The phrase "civilian support of military operations" inverts that vector

We have not been able to locate the phrase "civilian support of military operations" in DoDD 3025.18, JP 3-27 (*Homeland Defense*), JP 3-28 (*Defense Support of Civil Authorities*), 32 C.F.R. Part 185, the CRS Defense Primer on DSCA, or any National Guard Bureau instruction we have reviewed.

The phrase, as written, describes the **inverse** of DSCA — civilians supporting the military, rather than the military supporting civil authorities. This may be intentional and refer to a separate concept, or it may be a verbal inversion of DSCA. The candidates we considered for what the phrase *could* legitimately refer to are:

- **DoD Civilian Expeditionary Workforce (CEW)** under DoDI 1400.32 — federal DoD civilian employees designated as emergency-essential and deployable in support of contingency operations.
- **Contractors authorized to accompany the force (CAAF)** under DoDI 3020.41 and JP 4-10 — DoD contractors performing under federal contract in support of military operations.
- **Civil-Military Operations (CMO)** under JP 3-57 — primarily an overseas / stability-operations construct.

**None of those frameworks appears to reach an unpaid state defense force.** CEW personnel are federal employees; CAAF personnel are federal contractors; CMO is principally extraterritorial. We see no doctrinal pathway from any of these to TNSG.

### 2.3 Who is a "civilian" within DSCA?

Reading DoDD 3025.18 narrowly, the "DoD civilians" referenced in the DSCA definition are **federal civilian employees of DoD components** (i.e., personnel paid from DoD appropriations under federal civil service authorities). The DSCA definition also expressly enumerates "DoD contract personnel" as a separate category. The directive does *not* enumerate state employees, state defense force members, or private citizens.

This reading is reinforced by the funding architecture: DSCA is reimbursable, with reimbursement flowing between federal agencies (e.g., FEMA reimbursing DoD under the Stafford Act, 42 U.S.C. §§ 5121 *et seq.*; or under the Economy Act, 31 U.S.C. § 1535). There is no apparent reimbursement mechanism for a non-federally-funded volunteer state force, which suggests TNSG was never contemplated as a DSCA participant.

### 2.4 HD and DSCA are distinct mission frameworks

Per 10 U.S.C. § 101 and JP 3-27, **Homeland Defense** is defined as the protection of U.S. sovereignty, territory, domestic population, and critical defense infrastructure against external threats and aggression. DoD is the **lead** agency for HD.

Per JP 3-28, **DSCA** is the framework by which DoD provides support to civil authorities for domestic emergencies and other domestic activities. DoD is in a **supporting** role; civil authorities (typically a Lead Federal Agency such as FEMA, or state/local authorities) lead.

The CRS Defense Primer (IF11324) summarizes the distinction: DoD is the primary agency for HD activities and is typically a supporting agency for homeland security activities, of which DSCA is a subset. The relationship is illustrated in JP 3-27 Figure A-1.

The staff comment that DSCA is not the appropriate framework for an HD scenario therefore appears doctrinally correct: invoking DSCA presupposes a civil-authority lead, which is the inverse posture of an HD operation.

### 2.5 TNSG authority is grounded in state law, not federal DoD doctrine

**Federal authorization** for state defense forces is found at **32 U.S.C. § 109(c)**:

> In addition to its National Guard, if any, a State, the Commonwealth of Puerto Rico, the District of Columbia, Guam, or the Virgin Islands may, as provided by its laws, organize and maintain defense forces. A defense force established under this section may be used within the jurisdiction concerned, as its chief executive (or commanding general in the case of the District of Columbia) considers necessary, but it may not be called, ordered, or drafted into the armed forces.
>
> — 32 U.S.C. § 109(c)

Subsections (d) and (e) further specify that members of a § 109 defense force are not entitled to federal pay, allowances, subsistence, transportation, or medical care from federal funds, and that active reserve component members may not join.

**State authorization** in Tennessee is found at **TCA § 58-1-401**:

> Whenever the president of the United States shall call any part of the national guard of this state into active federal service, the governor is authorized to enlist, organize, maintain, equip and discipline a volunteer defense force, other than the national guard, to be known as the Tennessee state guard.
>
> — Tenn. Code Ann. § 58-1-401

We note — and flag for JAG — that the literal predicate in § 58-1-401 is presidential federalization of the Tennessee National Guard. The TNSG has nonetheless been continuously maintained as a standing force since its 1985 reactivation (as the Tennessee Defense Force, renamed in 1998), and is currently operating without any current federalization of the TNARNG/TNANG. JAG may wish to address whether the operative authority for present-day TNSG operations rests on (a) a broad reading of § 58-1-401, (b) a separate provision of TCA Title 58 we have not identified, (c) the Governor's residual militia authority under the Tennessee Constitution, or (d) some combination thereof.

**What this implies for TNMAN.** Reading these provisions together, the authority for TNSG to participate in TNMAN appears to flow exclusively from:

- The Tennessee Constitution's reservation of state military power,
- TCA Title 58 (state militia statutes), and
- The Adjutant General's directives executing the Governor's order;

with federal recognition under 32 U.S.C. § 109(c). DoDD 3025.18 does not list state defense forces among DSCA participants. We therefore do not see a federal DSCA pathway by which TNSG participates in TNMAN, and we do not see how DSCA — even if correctly invoked — would either authorize or constrain that participation.

A secondary consequence worth confirming: because TNSG operates exclusively as a state force and is never federalized, **the Posse Comitatus Act (18 U.S.C. § 1385) does not apply to TNSG** in any operational status. JAG may wish to confirm this for the planning record, since some of the staff discussion has assumed PCA-like constraints by analogy to active-duty federal forces.

## 3. Working Hypothesis

Our working hypothesis — offered tentatively, for JAG to confirm or reject — is the following:

> Somewhere in the planning process, a staff member referenced DSCA in connection with TNSG's role in TNMAN, either misunderstood the directionality of the DSCA framework or restated it imprecisely, and **verbally reversed the lead/supporting roles** — yielding the phrase "civilian support of military operations." That phrase was then absorbed into subsequent staff dialogue and treated as a source of authority for TNSG participation, rather than what it actually is: an inverted and undefined version of a federal doctrinal term that does not, in any event, reach a state defense force.

If that hypothesis is correct, the practical effect is that TNMAN planning materials may be implying federal authority for TNSG's role when the actual authority is purely state-based. That is a positioning error that could create avoidable friction in coordination with TNNG (Title 32) and any federal entities participating, and could create downstream problems if questions arise about status of forces, liability, workers' comp, or the chain of command during the exercise.

We would prefer to surface and correct that framing in the planning record now, before the exercise, rather than have it surface later.

## 4. What We Are Asking JAG to Do

1. **Confirm or correct** our reading of DSCA per DoDD 3025.18 and 32 C.F.R. Part 185.
2. **Confirm or correct** that "civilian support of military operations" is not a controlling doctrinal term and does not provide an authority basis for TNSG participation in TNMAN.
3. **Confirm or correct** that TNSG's authority for TNMAN participation rests on Tennessee Constitution + TCA Title 58 + 32 U.S.C. § 109(c), and not on any DSCA-derived authority.
4. **Address the § 58-1-401 predicate question** — whether the statute's "whenever the president … shall call any part of the national guard … into active federal service" predicate is interpreted as a triggering condition for the TNSG's existence/operation, or as historical phrasing now read more broadly in light of continuous TNSG operations since 1985.
5. **Advise on appropriate language** for the TNMAN planning record that correctly describes:
   - The mission framework (HD vs. DSCA vs. state militia operation),
   - TNSG's authority to participate, and
   - The chain-of-command and status-of-forces relationships among TNSG, TNARNG/TNANG (in whichever Title status), and any federal participants.
6. **Identify any other authorities** (state or federal) we should be citing in the TNMAN planning record that we have missed.
