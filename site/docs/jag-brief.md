# TNMAN JAG Consultation — Pre-Brief
**3rd Regiment, Tennessee State Guard**
**Prepared for: LTC Sheaf**
**Subject: Legal and Doctrinal Framework — Proposed TNMAN Mission Sets**

---

## Purpose

This document prepares regimental leadership for a structured, high-value consultation with our assigned JAG officer regarding 3RGT's proposed participation in TNMAN. The exercise has been framed as a **Homeland Defense (HD) scenario**, and 3RGT is expected to deploy to a National Guard Armory to conduct **SECFOR, surveillance, and UAS/sUAS missions**. That combination raises specific legal and doctrinal questions that JAG should address before the exercise proceeds.

---

## Background: The Doctrinal Friction Point

### HD vs. ICP — Why It Matters

The exercise is framed as **Homeland Defense (HD)** — a military mission with DoD as lead agency, operating under Title 10 authority through NORTHCOM. This is categorically distinct from:

- **DSCA (Defense Support of Civil Authorities)** — military in a supporting role to civilian lead
- **Emergency Response / Consequence Management** — which uses ICP/NIMS structures (FEMA framework)

If the scenario is genuinely HD, the doctrinally appropriate command structure is a **military staff model** (S1–S7 / J-staff), not an ICP. ICP is designed for multi-agency civilian incident management. Running an HD exercise through an ICP structure trains the wrong decision cycle, wrong reporting relationships, and wrong operational rhythm.

**Key question for exercise planners (not JAG, but worth documenting):** Has the exercise design authority explicitly resolved whether this is an HD scenario, a DSCA scenario, or a hybrid? The answer determines command structure, authority relationships, and which legal frameworks govern our operations.

## Legal Authority Framework

### Title 10 (Federal Armed Forces)

Title 10 governs all federal military forces — Regular components and federalized National Guard. Key characteristics:
- President as Commander-in-Chief through SecDef and combatant command structure
- NORTHCOM is the Title 10 command for HD missions on U.S. soil
- Posse Comitatus Act applies — no law enforcement functions
- Federal pay, benefits, and UCMJ

**HD is a Title 10 mission by definition.** In a real HD execution, NORTHCOM/federalized ARNG would own the main effort.

### Title 32 (National Guard — Hybrid Status)

National Guard units can operate under Title 32 for certain federal-funded missions while remaining under Governor command. This is the common mode for hurricane response, border operations, and similar domestic missions. ARNG units can slide between Title 32 and Title 10 depending on the activation authority.

### State Active Duty (SAD) — TNSG's Exclusive Status

**TNSG has no Title 10 existence and cannot be federalized.** We are purely a creature of Tennessee state law (TCA Title 58). Under SAD:
- The Governor is Commander-in-Chief
- Federal government has no command authority over TNSG assets
- Posse Comitatus does not apply — Tennessee state law governs instead
- State-funded; state-resourced

This creates a command authority seam in any HD-framed exercise: the Title 10 force (notional ARNG/federal) and the SAD force (TNSG) operate under fundamentally different legal authorities. **That seam must be explicitly documented in the exercise design — and TNSG personnel must not practice taking direction from notional federal commanders without a clear understanding that this would not be legally valid in a real execution.**

### TNSG's Realistic Role in an HD Scenario

Given our SAD status, TNSG would realistically serve in **enabling/economy-of-force roles**: security, surveillance, reconnaissance — functions that free Title 10 assets for the main effort while TNSG remains under Governor authority. The proposed TNMAN mission sets (SECFOR, surveillance, UAS) are consistent with this role. However, the authority basis for each must be explicitly grounded in Tennessee state law, not assumed from Army doctrine.

## Mission Set Analysis

### 1. SECFOR (Security Forces)

**Legal basis:** SAD authority under TCA Title 58 — providing security for state facilities and assets is well within historical TNSG employment.

**Key friction point — armory jurisdiction:** National Guard armories are typically state property, making TNSG SECFOR legally straightforward. However, armories commonly co-locate **federal property and Title 32 ARNG assets**. Whether TNSG SECFOR authority extends to those federal assets is not automatic and requires explicit resolution.

**ROE authority:** If SECFOR missions involve any law enforcement-adjacent actions — detaining individuals, controlling civilian movement, use of force — ROE must be written against **Tennessee state law authority**, not Army doctrine defaults. This is a common gap in Guard exercises that use Army ROE templates without verifying state law basis.

---

### 2. Surveillance

**Legal basis:** Information-gathering and situational awareness in support of the state mission is authorized under SAD. This is relatively clean doctrinal ground.

**Key friction point — Fourth Amendment and Tennessee privacy law:** Any surveillance that captures civilian activity — even incidentally — triggers both federal constitutional constraints and Tennessee statutory constraints on collection, retention, and dissemination.

**Key friction point — federal data sharing:** If surveillance products (reports, imagery, logs) are passed to federal partners (ARNG, NORTHCOM, TEMA federal liaisons), TNSG is potentially feeding a Title 10 intelligence apparatus from a state asset. This can trigger federal privacy frameworks (DoD 5240.1-R equivalent concerns) even though TNSG is not Title 10. The legal basis for that transfer must be established in advance.

---

### 3. UAS / sUAS Operations

This mission set carries the most legal layering and warrants the most JAG time.

**FAA / Part 107 layer:**
TNSG is not a Title 10 force and cannot operate under a military COA (Certificate of Authorization) from the FAA. Our operators must hold **FAA Part 107 certification** and all operations must comply with NAS rules. Depending on the armory's proximity to regional airports (Tri-Cities Regional, Greeneville, etc.), operations may fall within Class D or Class E airspace requiring ATCT/TRACON coordination prior to flight.

**Tennessee drone privacy statute (TCA 39-13-609):**
Tennessee law restricts certain aerial surveillance by government entities, even outside of criminal law enforcement contexts. The HD exercise framing does not automatically create an exception. JAG needs to determine whether our planned UAS missions require a specific authorization or fall within an existing exception under state law.

**Intelligence oversight:**
UAS-collected imagery is a surveillance product. If it depicts U.S. persons, Title 10 forces manage this under AR 381-10 equivalent rules — but we are not Title 10. Tennessee does not have a fully developed equivalent framework. JAG will largely be working from first principles here; this should be acknowledged going in.

**Equipment provenance:**
If UAS assets are ARNG-loaned or federally-owned equipment, there may be restrictions on TNSG employment of those assets under SAD authority. Ownership and terms of use must be confirmed before the equipment is committed to the exercise plan.

## Priority JAG Questions

Ranked by doctrinal significance and operational risk:

| # | Question | Mission Set | Risk if Unresolved |
|---|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| 1 | What is the explicit Tennessee state law authority citation for each proposed mission set (SECFOR, surveillance, UAS) under SAD? | All | Operations lack legal basis |
| 2 | How is the command relationship between TNSG and any ARNG / federal elements documented to preserve SAD authority and prevent de facto subordination to Title 10 command? | All | Erodes Governor's command authority; creates bad precedent |
| 3 | What are the ROE authorities and limits for SECFOR — specifically regarding civilian interaction, use of force, and extension of authority to federal property co-located at the armory? | SECFOR | Personnel exposure; unlawful detention risk |
| 4 | What retention, dissemination, and sharing rules govern surveillance and UAS imagery products generated under SAD authority — particularly if shared with federal partners? | Surveillance / UAS | Privacy law violations; federal intelligence oversight concerns |
| 5 | Does TCA 39-13-609 (Tennessee drone privacy statute) create operational restrictions on our UAS missions, and if so, what exception or authorization is available? | UAS | Statutory violation during exercise |
| 6 | If UAS equipment is federally owned or ARNG-loaned, what are the terms governing TNSG employment of that equipment under SAD? | UAS | Equipment use outside authorized terms |

## Recommended Posture for JAG Consultation

1. **Lead with the command authority question (Item 2).** It's the highest-order issue and frames everything else. If the exercise design hasn't resolved the Title 10 / SAD seam, we need that fixed before the mission set questions are even relevant.

2. **Bring the specific exercise OPORD or exercise design documentation** if available. JAG can give better answers against a specific document than against a hypothetical.

3. **Do not assume Army doctrine answers apply.** We are a SAD force. Every doctrinal answer must be grounded in Tennessee law or TCA Title 58, not FM references. JAG should be prepared to cite Tennessee authority, not Army authority.

4. **Document JAG answers in writing.** For an exercise of this scope, verbal guidance is insufficient. Request written legal opinions or at minimum a signed memo summarizing the authorized mission basis for each mission set.

5. **Flag the exercise design issue separately from the legal questions.** The HD vs. ICP command structure question is primarily an exercise design issue, not a JAG issue. It should be raised with the exercise OIC / G3 in parallel, not conflated with the legal consultation.
