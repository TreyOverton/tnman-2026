## Issue Presented

What legal authorities, restrictions, and best practices govern the use of UAS ("drones") by the Tennessee State Guard (TNSG) within the State of Tennessee?

## Short Answer

TNSG **may lawfully utilize drones** in support of its state mission. Use is constrained by:

1. Federal aviation regulations administered by the FAA — **14 C.F.R. Part 107**.
2. Tennessee criminal statutes, including **TCA §§ 39-13-902** (unlawful use of UAS) and **39-13-903** (imaging of critical infrastructure).

Drone use is permissible when tied to a legitimate governmental purpose, properly authorized, and conducted in a manner that avoids unlawful surveillance, intrusion upon privacy, or improper monitoring of critical infrastructure. **Improper use may expose personnel to criminal liability.**

## Applicable Law

### A. Federal Aviation Regulation

All UAS operations are subject to FAA regulation. Key requirements:

- **14 C.F.R. Part 107 (Small UAS Rule)** — applies to drones < 55 lbs:
  - Remote pilot certification
  - Visual line-of-sight operation; drone registration
  - Airspace restrictions and authorization (max 400 ft, 100 mph)
- **Public Aircraft Operations (PAO)** alternative — under 49 U.S.C. § 40102(a)(41) and § 40125, a State or political subdivision aircraft *may* qualify as a public aircraft when used for a governmental function. Qualifying as PAO removes Part 107 compliance requirements, **but**:
  - Safe operation standards still apply
  - ATC coordination may be required
  - FAA oversight is not eliminated
- **Privately-owned drones:** all Part 107 requirements remain in force regardless of mission status.

### B. Tennessee Criminal Statutes

**TCA § 39-13-902 — Unlawful Use of UAS** prohibits:

- Surveillance of individuals or private property without consent where there is a reasonable expectation of privacy
- Capturing images in a manner that intrudes upon private activities

**TCA § 39-13-903 — Imaging of Critical Infrastructure** prohibits:

- Capturing images of critical infrastructure facilities for surveillance or improper purposes

> **No express blanket exemption for the Tennessee State Guard.** These statutes apply to TNSG personnel unless the conduct falls within a lawful governmental function and is properly authorized.

## Practical Prohibitions

The following present significant legal risk and are generally prohibited absent clear authorization and justification:

1. **Surveillance of private property or individuals** — recording homes, backyards, individuals without consent; generalized neighborhood monitoring during training.
2. **Non-mission-based recording** — capturing or retaining imagery unrelated to a defined governmental objective; "training footage" that includes identifiable private individuals or property.
3. **Uncoordinated monitoring of critical infrastructure** — imaging power plants, substations, communications towers, similar facilities; reconnaissance without coordination.
4. **Data misuse** — retaining, sharing, or distributing collected imagery without a legitimate purpose; non-official use of drone footage.
5. **Unauthorized operations** — deployment outside the chain of command; operation in restricted airspace without FAA authorization.

## Allowable Use and Exceptions

Drone operations are generally permissible when **all** of the following are met (consistent with TCA § 39-13-902):

1. **Legitimate governmental purpose** — disaster response, search and rescue, emergency management support, training tied to mission readiness.
2. **Command authorization** — operations approved through chain of command; mission scope clearly defined and documented.
3. **Compliance with FAA requirements** — proper certification or qualification of operators; airspace authorization where required.
4. **Limited and purpose-driven data collection** — imaging confined to mission-essential objectives; avoidance of unnecessary capture of private individuals or property.
5. **Coordination where required** — engagement with civil authorities or infrastructure owners; deconfliction with law enforcement or emergency operations.

## Best Practices and Policy Recommendations

CPT Harris recommends:

1. **Adopt a formal UAS policy** — define permissible uses and prohibited conduct (recommend mirroring permitted/prohibited uses in TCA § 39-13-902); establish clear approval authority.
2. **Training and certification** — require FAA Part 107 (or equivalent); maintain operator training records and flight logs.
3. **Mission documentation** — written mission justification for each deployment; identify objectives, location, scope.
4. **Privacy safeguards** — prohibit intentional surveillance of private individuals absent lawful purpose; minimize incidental capture of private property.
5. **Data governance** — limit retention of imagery to mission-essential needs; establish deletion and access-control policies.
6. **Infrastructure coordination** — require approval prior to imaging sensitive facilities; coordinate with relevant agencies or operators.
7. **Legal review for sensitive operations** — JAG or command-level review for operations involving populated areas, surveillance concerns, or critical infrastructure.

## Conclusion

TNSG may effectively incorporate drone technology into its operations, but use must be carefully controlled. **TCA §§ 39-13-902 and 39-13-903** impose meaningful restrictions on surveillance and imaging; federal aviation law governs operational safety and airspace use.

The principal legal risks arise from unauthorized surveillance, improper data collection, and failure to coordinate or obtain necessary approvals. With appropriate policy controls, training, and command oversight, drone use can be conducted lawfully and in furtherance of the TNSG mission.
